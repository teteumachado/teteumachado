# TrueSkill — What I learned trying to understand how games rank players (and how it turned into a marketing project)

It all started with a silly curiosity: how does online game matchmaking ranking actually work?

Not the surface-level explanation of "you gain points when you win and lose them when you lose." I wanted to understand the mechanism underneath — why some games raise your rank even when you lose, why the system's uncertainty shrinks over time, why everything feels chaotic at the start of an account and then stabilizes.

When I dug deep, I found **TrueSkill** — a probabilistic algorithm created by Microsoft Research for ranking players on Xbox Live. And when I understood how it worked, I had one of those "wait, this isn't just for games" moments.

It works for marketing. It works really well.

---

## The problem I didn't know existed

Before I get to the solution, I need to tell you what the problem is.

There's a silent gap in almost every market preference research: the methods we use were built to measure objective things — conversion, clicks, revenue. When we apply them to understand _which creative people prefer_, or _which product name resonates more_, we're using the wrong tool for the wrong job.

Let me show you where each method breaks.

### A/B Testing

It's the darling of performance marketing, and for good reason — for final decisions with concrete metrics it's unbeatable. But when the goal is to _explore preferences across many items_, it has serious limitations:

- You test **two variants at a time**. To compare 10 creatives rigorously, you'd need dozens of sequential rounds.
- It requires **high traffic volumes** to reach statistical significance.
- It's **inherently slow** — each round needs exposure time and data collection.
- It doesn't produce a **global ranking**: it answers "A or B?", not "which of the ten is best?"

### Multi-Armed Bandits

Bandits are brilliant for **production optimization** — maximizing conversion in real time while still exploring. But they weren't built for structured research:

- The goal is to maximize performance, not _understand_ preference.
- They don't produce a clear, interpretable ranking at the end.
- The exploration is too dynamic for anyone who needs a structured answer to present to a client or team.

### Forms and scales (Likert, NPS)

Asking someone to rate a creative from 1 to 5 seems simple. The problem is it produces noisy data. People calibrate scales in completely different ways — one person's enthusiastic "4" is another's lukewarm "3". And comparing averages of items rated at different times is methodologically problematic.

---

## What TrueSkill does differently

TrueSkill is a **probabilistic ranking algorithm based on direct comparisons**. The premise is elegant:

> Instead of asking for absolute ratings, the system learns through comparisons — A or B? — and reconstructs a global ranking from those preferences.

This is exactly what happens in matchmaking. The system doesn't ask you to rate your skill from 1 to 10. It puts you in games against other players, observes who wins, and _infers_ your skill from those comparisons.

### Two numbers that carry everything

Each item in the system is represented by two parameters:

|Parameter|What it represents|
|---|---|
|**μ (mu)**|Estimated quality — position in the ranking|
|**σ (sigma)**|Uncertainty of that estimate — how much we still don't know|

This duo changes everything. It allows the system to distinguish completely different situations:

|Profile|What it means in practice|
|---|---|
|High μ + Low σ|Strong item, well validated, high confidence|
|High μ + High σ|Seems good, but hasn't been tested much|
|Low μ + High σ|Unknown — needs more comparisons before judging|
|Low μ + Low σ|Consistently weak — the system is certain|

A system that only stored μ would throw away critical information. The σ is what turns a static ranking into a living model that knows what it doesn't yet know.

### How learning happens

The flow from the user's perspective is simple:

```
User compares A vs B
        ↓
System records the preference
        ↓
TrueSkill updates μ and σ for both items
        ↓
Global ranking is recalculated
        ↓
System picks the next most informative pairs
```

With each comparison, the algorithm uses Bayesian inference to update its beliefs. Intuitively: if A beats B and B was considered strong, A's μ goes up significantly. If A beats an item already considered weak, the gain is smaller. The system weighs the information from each match.

And here's a detail few people mention: the system can **intelligently choose which pairs to compare**. Instead of testing every possible combination, it prioritizes the most informative matchups — where uncertainty is highest or where μ values are closest. The result: you rank dozens of items with a surprisingly small number of comparisons.

## Where it really shines — with real use cases

After understanding the algorithm, I started mapping where it makes the most sense. These are the contexts where TrueSkill clearly wins over traditional methods:

### 1. Creative triage before A/B testing

Your design team produced 15 variations of a banner. You don't have the budget or time to put them all in an A/B test. With TrueSkill, you show pairs to a small panel — 20, 30 people — and in just a few rounds you already have a ranking with confidence levels per item. The top two or three go into the real A/B test.

The algorithm worked as an **intelligent triage funnel**: cheap, fast, and based on real comparison instead of abstract rating.

### 2. Product or brand name research

Traditional naming research asks people to rate names on a 1–5 scale. The problem: people use scales differently, and comparing averages of items rated at different times is methodologically weak.

With TrueSkill, you simply ask "which of these two names resonates more with you?" repeatedly. The emerging ranking is far more reliable because it's _relative_ — the person doesn't need to calibrate an abstract scale, they just need to choose.

### 3. Product feature prioritization

Product teams often decide what to build through post-it voting or priority forms — both with serious bias and inconsistency issues. TrueSkill lets stakeholders make direct comparisons: "feature A or feature B is more important for the user?"

The result is a priority ranking with explicit uncertainty. Where σ is still high, you know exactly where you need more input before deciding.

### 4. Content curation with audience preference

A publication that wants to understand what its audience prefers — before having enough volume to train models — can use TrueSkill to collect preferences from a small group of readers and already have a robust ranking. Formats, topics, text depth, narrative approach — everything is rankable with just a few sessions.

### 5. Packaging or physical design selection

CPG industries spend fortunes on focus groups to choose packaging. TrueSkill doesn't eliminate the focus group, but it structures data collection much better within it. Instead of asking for open-ended ratings or scales, the facilitator conducts direct comparisons between prototypes. The result is an objective ranking that came out of a qualitative session.

## What TrueSkill answers — and what it doesn't

This point is important and I want to be honest about the limits.

TrueSkill does not answer: _"what is the absolute best?"_

It answers: _"based on the comparisons made so far, which item is most likely to be preferred?"_

It doesn't replace A/B testing when you need to measure real impact on conversion or revenue. It's not for optimizing ROAS in real time. TrueSkill lives in the **exploration and discovery** phase — before you have enough data and before you know which candidates are worth the investment of a definitive test.

The ideal flow is:

```
TrueSkill  →  preference ranking with little data
     ↓
Top 2-3 candidates selected
     ↓
A/B Testing  →  validation with real business metrics
```

Put that way, TrueSkill **lowers the cost of exploration** and **improves the quality of candidates** that reach the A/B test. The two methods don't compete — they feed each other.

## Where this is going

I participate in market research platforms, and it's always bothered me that the survey-taking experience is... lukewarm. 1–5 scales. Multiple choice questions. You answer, submit, and never know what happened to your opinion.

Direct comparisons are more engaging, more natural, and — as I showed above — produce better data. This is how we compare things in real life: not by rating items individually on abstract scales, but by putting options side by side.

I'm building a system based on TrueSkill to apply exactly this to preference research. The idea is that you upload creatives, ideas, or options, users respond to A vs B comparisons, and the system generates an automatic ranking with μ and σ for each item — with intelligent pair selection to minimize the number of comparisons needed.

The conceptual architecture:

```
User votes (A vs B)
        ↓
TrueSkill Engine  →  updates μ and σ
        ↓
Pair selection engine  →  next most informative comparisons
        ↓
Ranking with confidence per item
```

Before publishing the system, this article goes to the task list first — I want it to arrive together with the launch, so that whoever reads it can already understand the reasoning behind what was built.

In the next posts I'll detail the technical implementation: how the algorithm works in code, how to structure intelligent pair selection, and how to present the results clearly for non-statisticians.
